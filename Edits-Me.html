Rowan Vale

“On the road. Fixing what I can, leaving what I can’t. Cold coffee, good boots, dirt under my nails.”

About

Yeah, I learned pretty quick not to get too set on anything. People leave, places change, and if you hang on too hard, you just… end up dragging it with you. Dead weight. Seen it happen.

First thing that was really mine was this beat-up old van. Probably would’ve sat in some yard forever if I hadn’t grabbed it. I tore out the bad bits, put in cedar shelves, bed in the wall so I could move around without cracking my knees. Strung up some lights. Didn’t need them, I guess, but it felt better with ‘em.

Summers I’d be at markets or fairs. Sell some stuff, fix some stuff. Winters I took whatever came along. Patching roofs, fixing up cabins, cutting old wood till it sat the way it ought to. I liked that work. You could stand back after and think, yeah, that’s mine.

I don’t really go after trouble. No reason to. But once it’s on you, it’s hard to shake. Best way to stay breathing? Keep your ears open, notice when the wind shifts, and hear the boots before they’re close enough to matter.

Journal

August 7, 2007 — Louisville International Airport

Dead sockets. Warm air. Everyone waiting.

Lights blinked, then steadied. Somewhere far off, a siren didn’t know whether to commit. I counted steps between benches, picked out the families by their shoes. The coffee tasted like the inside of a penny. Kept the cup anyway; it fits the van holder just right.

July 29, 2007 — County line

New hinge hung true

Took three shims and a breath I didn’t know I was holding. Cedar sawdust in my hair again. Feels like being home.
